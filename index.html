Teacher Overview — Full Lesson Sequence
L
Launch — C–R–A Hook: The Covered Receipt (10 min)
🟡 Concrete
Give each pair a printed receipt (5 items, total covered with a sticky note). They have 30 seconds only to estimate the total — no calculators. They use play money rounded to the nearest dollar. Reveal the total. Discuss: how close were you? What strategy did you use?
🔵 Representational
Draw two number lines on the board: one showing $14.70 → rounds to $15, another showing $8.20 → rounds to $8. Connect the physical experience of rounding to the visual model. Ask: "What did your brain actually do when you estimated?"
This launches the open question for the Explore phase.
📌 Teacher Note — Don't skip the play money Even if students can round mentally, the physical act of separating coins to the nearest dollar builds embodied understanding of what "rounding" actually does to a number. This is the Concrete stage — it grounds the abstract rules that will come in the Summarise phase. Use receipts with a mix of amounts: some that round up, some that round down, so students encounter both directions.
⏱ 10 min
E
Explore — Budget Blitz (25 min)
Pairs receive the Budget Blitz item list (see Explore tab). They have a $50 budget and must decide: can they afford everything? Rule: they must estimate first (no calculators) and record their estimate, then calculate exactly and verify. No single estimation strategy is prescribed — pairs choose their own. Teacher circulates with probing questions only. Students grapple independently for at least 3–4 minutes before teacher support is offered.
📌 Circulation Questions — use these, not corrections "How did you estimate that one — did you round up or down?" / "Could your estimate be wrong by more than $5? Does that matter?" / "If you're $3 over budget in your estimate, are you definitely over, or might the exact total be under?" / "What's the worst case if all your estimates are slightly high?"
⏱ 25 min
S
Summarise — Naming Strategies + Abstract Formalisation (15 min)
🟢 Abstract
Groups share one strategy each. Teacher collects on the board without evaluating. Then formally name and define the three strategies. Write the abstract rule: "Estimation uses rounding rules to find an approximate value efficiently." Make explicit: estimation is a mathematical skill, not guessing — it has rules and structure. Pose the key question: "When is your estimate good enough? When do you need an exact answer?"
📌 Connecting to AC9M6N08 Use the standard's language explicitly: "approximate numerical solutions" and "appropriate estimation strategies." Ask: "Was rounding to the nearest dollar an appropriate strategy for this budget? What if the budget were $50.10?"
⏱ 15 min
R
Review — Exit Ticket + Mathematician's Reflection (10 min)
Two written responses and a Mathematician's Reflection. See Review tab. Collected by teacher — used to identify who needs more support with rounding before Lesson 5, and who is ready for the complex unfamiliar estimation extension.
⏱ 10 min

💬 Discuss with your partner

Talk through these questions before the class Summarise. Try to think of at least two or three different strategies your pair used — not just one.

Strategy Talk — Question 1

Did you add the estimates one by one from top to bottom — or did you find any pairs of prices that added to a whole number first? For example: $4.70 + $3.49 ≈ $5 + $3 = $8, but what if you spotted that 70c + 30c = $1 and grouped those two items together? Which prices in the list have cents that combine to make a whole dollar?

Strategy Talk — Question 2

Another strategy is column addition — writing all 12 estimates in a list and adding them vertically. Is this the most efficient strategy for mental estimation? What are its strengths and weaknesses compared to grouping compatible pairs first?

Strategy Talk — Question 3

A third strategy is chunking into groups of $10 or $20 — finding items that together cost close to $10, then counting how many groups you have. Could you do that with this list? Try grouping any three or four items whose estimates add to close to $10 or $20.

Strategy Talk — Question 4

How close was your estimate to the exact total? Was your estimate higher or lower? Think about why — did you tend to round up or round down more often? How does your rounding habit affect whether your estimate is a safe over-estimate or an unsafe under-estimate for a budget?

Summarise — Naming the Strategies
🟢 Stage 3: Abstract — Formalising

Each pair shares one strategy they used. Listen carefully — you don't have to have used the same one. The class will now name and define them.

📌 The Three Estimation Strategies
Strategy 1: Front-End Estimation
Round each price to the nearest dollar, then add them all up. Fast and easy — good for long shopping lists.
$4.70 → $5, $3.20 → $3, $8.90 → $9 … then add
Strategy 2: Leading Digit
Only look at the leading (first) digit of each price. Quick mental estimate — gives a rough lower bound.
$4.70 → $4, $8.90 → $8 (keep only the dollars)
Strategy 3: Compatible Numbers
Look for pairs of prices that add up neatly to $5, $10, or $20. Group them first, then add the groups.
$4.70 + $5.90 ≈ $5 + $6 = $11 (a compatible pair)
🟢 The Abstract Definition
Estimation uses rounding rules to find an approximate value efficiently.

Estimation is a mathematical skill, not guessing. It follows the same rules as rounding. A good estimate is close enough to be useful — but "close enough" depends on the context.

🔑 Answering the Open Question
✅ When an estimate is good enough
  • Checking if you can afford something before buying
  • Checking if your exact answer is reasonable
  • Quick mental checks at the shops
  • Deciding whether to use a calculator
⚠️ When you need an exact answer
  • Calculating GST on a tax invoice
  • Checking your change at the register
  • Working out a discount to the cent
  • Comparing two deals where the difference is small

E1 — Create a Reasonable-or-Not Card Set

Create 6 estimation claims — some that are reasonable, some that are clearly too high or too low. For each card write: the shopping context, the estimate made, and whether it's reasonable. A partner must sort your cards and justify each decision.

Example: "Mia estimated her $4.70 bread would round to $6. Is this reasonable?" (No — it should round to $5.)

Make some cards where the estimate is technically possible but still unreasonable for the context — e.g. rounding $4.70 to $4 is valid leading-digit estimation, but might it be too low for a budget check? The discussion is the point, not just sorting.
E2 — Maximum Item

Using the Budget Blitz list, you want to add a 9th item. Based on your exact total, what is the maximum you can spend on that item and still stay within $50?

Now: if you only had your estimated total to work from, what maximum would you have calculated? How does the difference affect your decision?

Exact total of the 12 items = $62.39
Maximum for 13th item (exact) = $80.00 − $62.39 = $17.61
If you used an estimate of $63, you'd calculate max = $17 — a conservative but safe choice. This shows that rounding up in estimates gives a safer budget buffer.

Deep ChallengeComplex Unfamiliar — systematic error analysis
A StandardComprehensive reasoning about estimation error in complex unfamiliar situations.
★1 — Always Round Up vs Always Round Down

Imagine a shopper who always rounds up to the nearest dollar. Another always rounds down. Both are shopping with a list of 20 items, each priced between $1.00 and $15.99.

What is the maximum possible estimation error for each strategy? Express your answer as a dollar amount and as a percentage of the total. Which strategy gives a safer budget estimate and why?

Think about a single item first. When you round a price to the nearest dollar, what is the largest amount you could be wrong by? Now think about what happens when you repeat that across 20 items — does the error stay the same, add up, or could it cancel out? What is the worst-case scenario?
Once you have a maximum error in dollars, think about how you would express that as a percentage of the total. What does the total depend on — and how would you estimate it from the scenario given? Consider how the two strategies (always up vs nearest dollar) would compare on that measure.
Always round down (leading digit): max error = up to $0.99 per item × 20 = $19.80. If average item is ~$8, total ≈ $160. Error as % ≈ 12.4%.
Always round to nearest: max error = $0.50 per item × 20 = $10. Same total → error ≈ 6.25%.
For a budget check, round UP is safest — it ensures you never underestimate and arrive at the checkout short. Rounding to nearest is a good balance of speed and accuracy.
Key insight: the estimation strategy you choose should match the consequence of being wrong.
